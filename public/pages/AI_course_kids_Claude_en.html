Section 1

🧠 What Is an LLM?

LLM stands for Large Language Model — it's the engine inside all AI chatbots. Imagine someone who read every book in every library, every website on the internet, and millions of articles — and then got really good at guessing what tiny piece of text comes next. That's basically an LLM.

It's more like a super-powered autocomplete than a thinking brain — it predicts the most likely next piece of text, over and over, until it has a full answer. Whether it truly "understands" things is actually a deep philosophical question that even scientists disagree about!

👍 AI is great at…

  • Explaining things in simple words
  • Writing and editing text
  • Translating languages (though not perfect!)
  • Answering common questions
  • Helping write or fix code
  • Brainstorming ideas

👎 AI struggles with…

  • Maths — the language model itself is bad at it (many chatbots now plug in a real calculator to help)
  • Very recent news or events
  • Consistently knowing when it is wrong
  • Precise numbers and dates
  • Hard multi-step problems
  • Things that need real common sense
⚠️

Watch out: Hallucinations!

Sometimes AI completely makes up "facts" that sound totally real and confident. Scientists call this hallucination. For example, it might invent a fake book title and tell you it's a classic. Always double-check anything important — especially numbers, dates, and sources!

🎮 MINI GAME — WHAT IS A TOKEN?

The LLM doesn't work with whole words — it works with tokens, which are small chunks of text (roughly ¾ of a word each). "Photosynthesis" might be two tokens: "Photo" + "synthesis". At every step, the model picks the most probable next token.

💬 What Is a Chatbot?

If an LLM is the engine, a chatbot is the cockpit you sit in. The LLM is doing all the heavy work hidden away — the chatbot is the screen and keyboard that lets you actually talk to it.

You type a message (called a prompt), and the chatbot sends it — along with some hidden instructions — to the LLM. The LLM generates a reply token by token, and it streams back to your screen as it goes.

🛠️

Chatbots can use tools!

Modern chatbots don't just talk — they can also use tools, like a web search engine, a calculator, or a code runner. The LLM decides when a tool is needed, uses it, reads the result, and then writes its reply. This means a chatbot can answer questions about things that happened yesterday, not just things it learned during training.

🔒 Privacy — What NOT to Share

Every message you type into a chatbot travels across the internet to a company's server. Think of it less like a private diary, and more like a letter you send to a company — they receive it, store it, and depending on their policies, staff may review it or it may be used to train the next version of the AI.

The golden rule: Would you be okay if a stranger on the street could read this? If not — don't type it into a chatbot.

🚫 Never share

  • Your full name + home address
  • Passwords or PINs
  • Photos of yourself or friends
  • Your school name + personal details
  • Your phone number
  • Anything a stranger shouldn't know

✅ Generally fine

  • School subject questions
  • Help writing a story or essay
  • Explaining science concepts
  • Brainstorming ideas
  • Practising a language
  • General "how does X work?" questions
💡

Useful trick: use fake names in examples

If you need the AI to help with something personal — like writing a message to a friend — just use a made-up name. Instead of "My friend Sofia...", try "My friend Alice...". You get the same help without sharing real details about real people.

✨ The 4-D Framework

Before asking an AI to do anything, run through these 4 steps. Think of them like a checklist before a rocket launch — skip a step and things go sideways! This framework was created by the people who build Claude.

🎮

Real example: homework help

Delegate: "I want the AI to explain photosynthesis — not write my essay for me." Describe: "Explain it for a 12-year-old, in 3 sentences, using a food analogy." Discern: Does this match what my textbook says? Diligence: Write it in your own words — never just copy the AI's answer and hand it in!

✍️ How to Talk to AI (Prompting)

A prompt is what you type to the AI. Here's the key thing: the AI takes your message super literally. It won't guess what you meant — it only works with what you actually wrote. Vague question = vague answer.

It's like ordering at a restaurant. If you just say "food please", you might get anything. But if you say "a veggie pizza, medium size, no olives" — you get exactly what you want!

1

Say who you are and why you're asking

"I'm a 12-year-old learning about volcanoes for a school project. Explain how magma forms using a simple analogy."

2

Tell it exactly what format you want

Say "2 sentences", "a bullet list of 5 points", "explain like I'm 10". Otherwise you might get three pages when you needed one paragraph!

3

The first answer is just a draft — keep going!

Follow up: "Make it shorter", "Give me a different example", "Explain the last part again differently". That's completely normal — the best results come from back-and-forth.

4

Always verify important facts yourself

Numbers, dates, names, scientific facts — never trust these without looking them up. The AI sounds confident even when it's completely wrong!

🤯

Myth: "I corrected the AI, so now it knows better!"

Nope! Correcting a chatbot only updates things in that one conversation. Close the tab and it forgets everything — it goes right back to its old ways. The AI only truly learns when its developers train a new version, which takes months.

📦 Context Rot — Why Long Chats Go Weird

Every AI conversation lives inside a context window — think of it like a backpack that can only hold so many notes. The AI reads every note in the backpack to answer your question. As the conversation grows, new notes get added. When the backpack is full and a new note needs to go in, the oldest note falls out completely — not slowly, but instantly gone.

The AI doesn't tell you it forgot. It just quietly starts ignoring instructions from the start of the chat, or contradicts things it said earlier. This gradual slip in quality is called context rot.

🔴 Signs of Context Rot

  • Contradicts what it said earlier
  • Forgets your formatting rules
  • Brings back old mistakes you fixed
  • Answers start feeling off or generic

🟢 What to Do

  • Start a brand new conversation
  • Paste a short summary instead of the whole chat
  • Repeat key rules near your latest message
  • Break big tasks into smaller pieces
🎒

Think of it like a school backpack

The context window is like a backpack with limited space. New notes go in at the front. When it's completely full and you add a new note, the oldest note at the back falls out entirely — it doesn't fade, it just disappears. The AI can only "see" the notes currently in the backpack. Once a note falls out, the AI has no memory of it at all.

Section 9

🛡️ What to Trust — and What to Check

Not everything an AI says is equally reliable. Some outputs are safe to use right away — others you should always check independently first. Think of it like a traffic light.

🎯

The AI doesn't always know what it doesn't know

A tricky thing about AI is that it can sound equally confident whether it's right or wrong. A careful person who's unsure will say "I think…" or "I'm not 100% certain…" — and AI chatbots do sometimes do this too, but not reliably enough to trust. For anything important — a fact in your essay, a date in a project, a statistic — always verify with a real source like your textbook, a library book, or a trustworthy website. Don't assume that because the AI sounds confident, it's correct.
